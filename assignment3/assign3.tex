\section{\textbf{PROBLEM STATEMENT}}
For the matrix $\vec{A}$ = \myvec{3&2\\1&1},find the numbers a and b such that $\vec{A}^2+a\vec{A}+b\vec{I} = 0$.

\section{\textbf{Theory}}
Cayley-Hamilton Theorem : A matrix satisfies its own characteristic equation.That is if the characteristic equation of an $n\times n$ matrix $\vec{A}$ is
\begin{align}
  \lambda^n+a_{n-1}\lambda^{n-1}+...+a_1\lambda+a_0 &= 0,then\nonumber\\
  \vec{A}^n+a_{n-1}\vec{A}^{n-1}+...+a_1\vec{A}+a_0\vec{I} &= 0\nonumber
\end{align}
\section{\textbf{Solution}}
For a general square matrix $\vec(A)$, the characteristic equation in variable $\lambda$  is defined by
\begin{align}
  \det({\vec{A}-\lambda \vec{I}}) &= 0\\
  \implies\mydet{3-\lambda&2\\1&1-\lambda} &= 0\\
  \implies(3-\lambda)(1-\lambda)-2 &= 0\\
  \implies\lambda^2-4\lambda+3-2 &= 0\\
  \implies\lambda^2-4\lambda+1 &= 0\label{eq1}
\end{align}
Now by Cayley-Hamilton Theorem $\vec{A}$ satisfies $\eqref{eq1}$,then replacing $\lambda$ with $\vec{A}$ we get
\begin{align}
  \vec{A}^2-4\vec{A}+\vec{I} &= 0\label{eq2}
\end{align}
We need to find a and b in
\begin{align}
  \vec{A}^2+a\vec{A}+b\vec{I} &= 0\label{eq3}
\end{align}
Comparing $\eqref{eq2}$ and $\eqref{eq3}$ we get a = -4 and b = 1.
